### Research Interest
My long-term goal is to understand how brain states emerge, transition, and become dysregulated across health and disease. By integrating clinical medicine, electrophysiology, and mathematical modeling, I seek to uncover the principles that govern interactions between sleep, mental health, and metabolism.

I am currently a clinical resident at Kurume University Hospital while pursuing doctoral training in Systems Pharmacology at the Graduate School of Medicine, The University of Tokyo, and conducting research in systems biology at the Kurume University Institute of Molecular Life Science. My primary research focuses on mathematical modeling of sleep and neuronal dynamics. In particular, I investigate how brain states such as sleep and wakefulness are generated, maintained, and transitioned through interactions among neuronal firing patterns, ion dynamics, membrane excitability, and network connectivity.

Sleep research is both the goal and the methodological foundation of my scientific career. Sleep represents one of the few biological phenomena in which neural activity, behavior, and physiological regulation can be quantitatively observed across multiple scales. For this reason, I view sleep as an ideal model system for studying the fundamental principles of brain-state transitions. My objective is not only to understand sleep itself, but also to use sleep as a window into broader mechanisms that govern brain function and disease.

Using this framework, I have developed mathematical models of sleep-related neuronal activity, focusing on Na⁺ dynamics as a key regulator of up–down firing patterns and slow-wave oscillations. Rather than treating sleep as a static behavioral state, I conceptualize it as a dynamic process emerging from interactions among neuronal activity, membrane potentials, ionic concentrations, and neural circuits. More recently, my work has expanded from single-neuron dynamics toward network-level mechanisms underlying state transitions in the sleeping brain.

I am particularly interested in applying insights from sleep research to psychiatry. Many neuropsychiatric disorders, including major depression, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia, are closely associated with abnormalities in sleep architecture and neural activity. However, the underlying dynamical principles linking these phenomena remain poorly understood. I hope to reinterpret psychiatric disorders not only as collections of symptoms, but also as disorders of brain-state regulation. By studying how neural systems transition between stable and unstable states, I aim to contribute to a mechanistic understanding of psychiatric disease grounded in neural dynamics.

At the same time, I am interested in the interactions between brain states and metabolism. Sleep disturbances are strongly associated with obesity, diabetes, and metabolic dysfunction, suggesting that the brain and peripheral organs operate as components of an integrated regulatory system. Rather than viewing psychiatric and metabolic disorders as separate entities, I seek to understand them as different manifestations of dysregulated biological state control.

This perspective also shapes my clinical research. In endocrinology and metabolism, I have analyzed continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) data as time-series signals, quantifying dynamic responses to pharmacological treatment and surgical intervention. By integrating clinical observations with electrophysiological principles and mathematical models of β-cell activity, I have investigated how alterations in cellular responsiveness contribute to disease dynamics. Similarly, in nephrology-related research, I have explored the mechanisms of LDL apheresis by reconstructing treatment effects as dynamic interactions among lipid metabolism, receptor regulation, and pharmacokinetics. Although these projects involve different clinical fields, they share a common objective: understanding biological systems through their dynamic behavior over time.

Across all of my work, a central question remains constant: How are biological states defined, how do they transition, and how can they be controlled? Whether studying neuronal activity during sleep, psychiatric symptoms, glucose fluctuations, or therapeutic responses, I view these phenomena as observable manifestations of underlying dynamical processes operating across multiple biological scales.

Moving forward, I aim to further integrate computational neuroscience, sleep research, psychiatry, and metabolism. By combining mathematical models of neuronal dynamics with clinical time-series datasets—including electrophysiological recordings, sleep measurements, hormonal profiles, and metabolic data—I hope to identify common principles governing state regulation in the human body and brain.

Ultimately, my goal is to become a physician-scientist who bridges sleep science, psychiatry, and metabolic medicine. Through quantitative approaches that connect molecular mechanisms, neural circuits, physiology, and clinical phenotypes, I hope to establish a unified framework for understanding how biological states emerge, transition, and become disrupted in human disease.
