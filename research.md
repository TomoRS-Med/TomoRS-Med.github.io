### Research Interest
I aim to understand the principles governing state transitions in biological systems by integrating clinical medicine and mathematical modeling.

I am currently a clinical resident at Kurume University Hospital, while also pursuing doctoral training in Systems Pharmacology at the Graduate School of Medicine, The University of Tokyo, and conducting research in systems biology at the Kurume University Institute of Molecular Life Science. My primary research focus is on mathematical modeling of sleep and neuronal dynamics. In particular, I seek to describe how brain states such as sleep and wakefulness are generated, maintained, and transitioned, based on neuronal firing patterns and ion dynamics.

In parallel with my clinical training, I reinterpret clinical cases in endocrinology, metabolism, nephrology, and drug response not as isolated observations, but as dynamic systems represented by time-series data. My research interest is not confined to specific organs or diseases; rather, I aim to develop a unified framework to understand diverse phenomena—including sleep, neuronal activity, glucose dynamics, pharmacological responses, and renal function—as time-evolving biological states.

My research approach is consistent across domains. First, I treat observed phenomena as time-series data. Second, I identify dynamic features that are not captured by conventional clinical metrics. Third, I construct mechanistic hypotheses grounded in electrophysiology, pharmacokinetics, and molecular biology. Finally, I formalize these hypotheses into mathematical models to achieve a reproducible and quantitative understanding. This framework is shared between my basic and clinical research.

In my work on sleep and neuronal dynamics, I have developed mathematical models focusing on Na⁺ dynamics to describe up–down firing patterns and slow-wave oscillations. Rather than treating sleep as a static behavioral or electrophysiological category, I conceptualize it as a dynamic state emerging from interactions among neuronal activity, membrane potentials, ion concentrations, and network connectivity.

In my clinical research, I have worked on reconstructing disease dynamics as mechanistic systems based on time-series data, rather than treating clinical observations as static endpoints. For example, I analyzed glucose dynamics in an insulinoma case using continuous glucose monitoring (CGM), treating glucose levels as time-series signals rather than static measurements. In addition to conventional metrics such as TIR, TBR, TAR, and CV, I introduced indices capturing the rate and directionality of glucose changes, including fasting glucose decline and postprandial response. By analyzing changes before and after diazoxide treatment and surgical resection, I interpreted differences between normal β-cells and tumor cells in terms of electrophysiological responsiveness. These findings were further connected to existing β-cell models, linking clinical observations with mathematical representations. In another study, I investigated the mechanism of LDL apheresis in a case of treatment-resistant nephrotic syndrome. Instead of reporting clinical efficacy alone, I reconstructed the system in terms of interactions among LDL, cyclosporine, LDL receptors, and intracellular drug uptake. I developed a model incorporating parameters such as plasma LDL levels, receptor activity, binding properties, and membrane permeability, and performed parameter sweeps and sensitivity analyses. This allowed me to interpret the reduction in proteinuria as a dynamic system-level change arising from the interaction between lipid metabolism and pharmacokinetics. Although these studies span different clinical domains, they are all driven by a single central question: how biological states are defined, how they transition, and how they can be controlled. Neuronal activity during sleep, glucose fluctuations, pharmacological responses, and renal function changes can all be understood as observable manifestations of underlying state transitions emerging from multi-scale biological interactions.

Based on this perspective, I define my research theme as the study of biological state transitions through mathematical modeling and clinical time-series data.

Moving forward, I plan to further integrate basic and clinical research by linking models of neuronal dynamics with clinical datasets, including CGM, hormonal profiles, and sleep measurements. I am particularly interested in quantifying state transitions before and after interventions such as drug administration or surgery. Sleep and metabolism share common regulatory structures and timescales, and their joint analysis may provide a pathway toward an integrated understanding of body–brain dynamics.

My work is not limited to any single disease or organ system. By combining mathematical modeling with real-world clinical data, I aim to establish a framework for describing and understanding biological state transitions in a unified and quantitative manner.
