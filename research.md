### Research Interest
My long-term goal is to understand the principles governing brain-state transitions and to apply these insights to psychiatric and metabolic disorders. By integrating clinical medicine, electrophysiology, and mathematical modeling, I seek to establish a quantitative framework that links neural dynamics, physiology, and disease.

I am currently a clinical resident at Kurume University Hospital while pursuing doctoral training in Systems Pharmacology at the Graduate School of Medicine, The University of Tokyo, and conducting research in systems biology at the Kurume University Institute of Molecular Life Science. My primary research focuses on mathematical modeling of sleep and neuronal dynamics. In particular, I investigate how brain states such as sleep and wakefulness are generated, maintained, and transitioned through interactions among neuronal firing patterns, ion dynamics, membrane excitability, and network connectivity.

Sleep research is both the goal and the methodological foundation of my scientific career. Sleep is one of the few biological phenomena in which neural activity, behavior, and physiological regulation can be quantitatively observed across multiple scales. For this reason, I regard sleep as an ideal model system for studying the fundamental principles of brain-state transitions. My aim is not only to understand sleep itself, but also to use sleep as a window into broader mechanisms governing brain function and disease.

In my work on sleep and neuronal dynamics, I have developed mathematical models focusing on Na⁺ dynamics to describe up–down firing patterns and slow-wave oscillations. Rather than treating sleep as a static behavioral or electrophysiological category, I conceptualize it as a dynamic state emerging from interactions among neuronal activity, membrane potentials, ionic concentrations, and neural circuits. More recently, my work has expanded from single-neuron dynamics toward network-level mechanisms underlying state transitions in the sleeping brain.

A major motivation for this work is its relevance to psychiatry. Many psychiatric disorders, including major depression, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia, are closely associated with abnormalities in sleep architecture and neural activity. However, the dynamical principles linking these phenomena remain poorly understood. I hope to use insights gained from sleep research to reinterpret psychiatric disorders as disorders of brain-state regulation. By studying how neural systems transition between stable and unstable states, I aim to contribute to a mechanistic understanding of psychiatric disease grounded in neural dynamics rather than symptom-based classification alone.

Changes in brain states also extend beyond mental function and profoundly influence physiological homeostasis throughout the body. Sleep and wakefulness regulate energy balance and glucose metabolism through interactions with autonomic, endocrine, and metabolic systems. From this perspective, the brain and body do not operate as separate entities, but as components of an integrated regulatory network. I therefore seek to understand sleep and psychiatric function not as isolated cerebral phenomena, but as processes embedded within whole-body physiology.

This perspective naturally extends my interests toward metabolism. While sleep disturbances are known to increase the risk of obesity, diabetes, and metabolic dysfunction, I am interested in understanding these relationships not merely as epidemiological associations, but as interactions between mechanisms governing brain-state regulation and metabolic control. In my view, sleep, mental health, and metabolism represent interconnected manifestations of a common system responsible for maintaining biological state stability.

This framework also shapes my clinical research. In endocrinology and metabolism, I have analyzed continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) data as time-series signals, quantifying dynamic responses to pharmacological treatment and surgical intervention. By integrating clinical observations with electrophysiological principles and mathematical models of β-cell activity, I have investigated how alterations in cellular responsiveness contribute to disease dynamics. Rather than viewing metabolic disorders solely through static measurements, I seek to understand them as changes in the dynamics of biological control systems.

Similarly, in nephrology-related research, I have explored the mechanisms of LDL apheresis by reconstructing treatment effects as dynamic interactions among lipid metabolism, receptor regulation, and pharmacokinetics. Although these projects span different clinical fields, they share a common objective: understanding biological systems through their dynamic behavior over time.

Across all of my work, a central question remains constant: How are biological states defined, how do they transition, and how can they be controlled? Whether studying neuronal activity during sleep, psychiatric symptoms, glucose fluctuations, or therapeutic responses, I view these phenomena as observable manifestations of underlying dynamical processes operating across multiple biological scales. To me, mathematical modeling is not merely an analytical tool, but a common language for understanding complex biological systems.

Moving forward, I aim to further integrate computational neuroscience, sleep research, psychiatry, and metabolism. By using sleep as a model system to uncover the principles of brain-state transitions, I hope to apply these insights to psychiatric and metabolic disorders and identify mechanisms shared across seemingly distinct diseases. I am particularly interested in connecting neuronal activity data from basic research with clinical time-series datasets, including sleep measurements, hormonal profiles, and metabolic monitoring, thereby creating a bidirectional bridge between basic and clinical science.

Ultimately, I aspire to become a physician-scientist who uses insights derived from sleep research to understand the biological principles linking the brain and body. By integrating molecular mechanisms, neural circuits, physiology, behavior, and clinical phenotypes, I hope to establish a unified framework for understanding how biological states emerge, transition, and become disrupted in human disease.
